The Dish

The slowest cooking in your kitchen.
Now ours.

There are dishes that ask you to wait at the stove. Bhuna asks you to walk away. Onions melt into the iron of the pan, dark and slow, until they no longer look like onions. The meat — beef or mutton, chosen that morning, never frozen — is laid into that sweetness and turned, slowly, until each piece carries the colour of the pan. Then the masala: roasted, pounded, folded in spoonful by spoonful, bhunaoed — that's the word — until the spices break their oil and the kitchen smells of something older than the building you're standing in. We lower the flame. We close the lid. We do not open it again until morning.

Eight hours, minimum. No exceptions.

The bhuna begins in the afternoon and ends after the sun comes up. No pressure cookers, no shortcuts, no rushing the moment when the oil rises and the masala turns glossy. The pot is left on its lowest flame through the night so the gravy reduces of its own accord — slowly, completely — until it stops pouring and begins to cling.

The meat is half the dish.

Fresh beef at AED 185/kg, mutton at AED 205/kg — sourced from Meat One, airlifted, never frozen, never thawed. We hand-pick cuts that survive eight hours on the flame and still come apart with the side of a spoon. Tough cuts cooked properly are kinder than tender cuts cooked fast.

To bhuna is to know.

The word bhuna doesn't translate. It means to fry the masala — slowly, attentively, with intention — until the spices, oil and meat become one substance. It is the moment everyone else skips. We don't. It takes the time it takes, and we time nothing else by it.

Built for the mop-up.

A dry, glazed bhuna asks for soft, blistered naan — that's how it's eaten across the old kitchens of South Asia. Paratha, rumali, or a plain rice roti work just as well. Eat it with your hands. It tastes more honest that way.

The Heritage

A dish older than the country it comes from.

Bhuna Gosht doesn't have an inventor. It has a lineage.

Its earliest grandparents come from the royal kitchens of the Mughals — cooks who arrived in the Indian subcontinent in the sixteenth century, bringing with them the Central Asian instinct to cook meat in its own fat. They called it bhunna in Persian — to fry, to roast, to coax. Over four hundred years it travelled. Through Awadh and the slow, perfumed kitchens of Lucknow. Through Hyderabad's deg pots. Down into Mumbai's old Muslim quarters of Mohammed Ali Road and Bohri Mohalla, where bhuna became the dinner of the night — never lunch — eaten after the day was finally over.

In Ramadan, every house had its pot. Every grandmother had her version. Every kitchen had the same sound: the soft hiss of a lid that hadn't been lifted in hours.

Spice Haus is a love letter to that pot. One recipe. The old method. The same patience. We didn't invent any of this. We are simply unwilling to let it disappear.

How it Works

Three steps. The fourth is patience.

01

Order & sourcing

You tell us your kilos, your meat, and the night you want it. We confirm on WhatsApp between 9 AM and 5 PM. Twenty-four hours is the minimum — not a policy, a requirement. As soon as the order is locked, the meat is reserved from Meat One for the next morning's pickup.

02

Caramelise & bhuna

The onions go in first. They cook longer than most people will believe — until they are no longer beige, no longer brown, but the deep mahogany of old wood. The meat joins them. The masala follows. We pound, we fold, we stir slowly. We wait for the oil to rise.

03

Overnight & delivered

The pot is sealed. The flame is dropped to a whisper. Through the night, the gravy gives up its water, the meat gives up its resistance, and the spices give up their last secrets. In the morning, we pack it hot and bring it to you across Sharjah, Ajman and Dubai — or you collect it from the kitchen, still warm.

From Our Kitchen

A small kitchen. One dish. Done properly.

Spice Haus is not a restaurant. There is no menu to flip through, no twelve curries vying for your attention. We make one thing, and we make it slowly. A single, honest dish — Bhuna Gosht — handed down through four centuries of cooks who refused to hurry it.

We cook in small batches. Each kilo is made for the person who ordered it, in the quantity they asked for, on the night they wanted it. Nothing is reheated. Nothing waits in a freezer for a buyer. That is why we ask for twenty-four hours: so what arrives at your door tomorrow is being caramelised, right now, today.

Some recipes shouldn't be made faster. Bhuna is one of them. The dish has waited four hundred years. It can wait one more day.
